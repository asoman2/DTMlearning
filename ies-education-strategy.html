UX team education strategy · Benefit-led · Cognitively load-sequenced

Education that leads with
"here's what changes for you"

The heaviest migrations fail not because people can't learn the software — but because nobody convinced them the new way was worth learning. This strategy reverses the sequence: show the benefit first, then teach the minimum needed to reach it. Complexity arrives only after motivation is established.

The governing principle
"Never ask someone to learn something before they understand why their life will be better because of it."
Pre-migration
Curiosity, not anxiety
Show the destination. Let people see what IES looks like from their perspective before any change is asked of them.
During migration
Just-in-time, not just-in-case
Education arrives exactly when the action is required. Nobody reads a manual before they need it — and nobody should have to.
Post go-live
Capability build, not catch-up
Day 1 teaches survival. Day 30 teaches efficiency. Day 90 teaches transformation. Complexity earns the right to arrive.
00 — The motivation problem — why education fails
Root cause

Migration education typically fails because it is framed as a compliance requirement rather than a capability unlock. Training sessions say "here is how to enter a bill in IES." They do not say "here is why you will spend 3 fewer hours per week on bill entry after you learn this." The first framing activates resistance. The second activates interest. The UX team's primary job is not content creation — it is motivation architecture. Build the "why this benefits me" before the "how to do it" for every persona, every touchpoint, every phase.

01 — Pre-migration phase — curiosity before anxiety
Months –6 to –1
Phase framing · Pre-migration
The goal is not readiness. The goal is excitement.
Pre-migration education should not feel like training. It should feel like a preview. Show people what their IES experience looks like from their perspective — before asking them to do anything. A Project Manager who has seen a real-time job cost dashboard before go-live is motivated. One who has sat through a 2-hour "IES overview" webinar is resentful. The experience of the destination must precede the instruction about the journey.
Month –6
Programme start

Leadership "Day in the life" preview — IES from the CFO's chair
A 20-minute live session for CFO and Division VPs showing a working IES environment — not a features tour, but a scenario: "It's 7am Monday. Here is what you see before your first coffee." Real-time consolidated P&L across all branches. A job cost alert on a project running 8% over budget. The 90-day cash forecast. No slides, no buzzwords — just the CFO's Monday morning, improved.
"I will never wait for a Monday Excel file again." — The insight that motivates the CFO to champion the migration to their team.

02 — During migration — just-in-time, not just-in-case
Go-live weeks
Phase framing · During migration
The worst time to teach is when someone is already scared.
The moment a system switches from familiar to unfamiliar, people experience a form of threat response. Their cognitive availability for learning drops dramatically. During-migration education must be embedded in the action, not delivered as a separate activity. The help appears when and where the action happens. Questions are answered by a person — the Champion — not a ticket system. And the tone is always: "You've got this. Here's exactly what to do next."
Go-live day
Day 0
During migration · First login
Personalised welcome screen — "Here's what you do first"
The first IES login shows a role-specific welcome screen. Not a feature tour. One sentence that names the benefit: "Welcome to IES — where you'll find your job cost in real time, not at month-end." Below that: three next actions specific to their role. PM: "Find your first project." Payroll: "Check your employees are here." Field: "Download your first pay stub." No homepage clutter. No notifications. Three tasks, sequenced from easiest to most important.
"I know exactly what to do first. I don't feel lost." — The first 5 minutes determine whether someone's relationship with IES is positive or anxious.

Days 1–3
First actions
During migration · Contextual help
In-product tooltips and "Why is this here?" hover explanations
Every IES screen element that replaces a familiar QBDT concept has a hover tooltip explaining the translation: "This is IES Projects — it works like your QBDT Customer:Jobs, but you can see your budget vs actual cost in real time." Concepts are introduced in QBDT language first, IES language second. The tooltip appears once, automatically, the first time that element is encountered. It does not appear again unless the user asks for it. Contextual help is not a popup at login — it is a whisper at the moment of confusion.
"I don't need to find the manual. The explanation is right where I'm looking." — Contextual help eliminates the most common Day 1 question: "Where did X go?"

Day 3–5
During migration · Critical moment
Payroll admin guided walkthrough — first live run with a co-pilot
The Payroll Manager does not run the first live IES payroll alone. An IPS payroll specialist is on a screen-share call for the entire first payroll run. Not to take over — to be present. Week 2–4
Stabilisation
During migration · Error recovery
Mistake-friendly environment — "Here's how to undo that" over "You shouldn't have done that"
Every IES error message encountered by Meridian users during the first month is logged by the Champion and reviewed weekly with the PS team. The top 5 errors become educational content: a short "how this happened and how to resolve it" note sent to all Champions. Not framed as "users were doing it wrong" — framed as "here's a situation that comes up, here's what to do." The culture of the migration must be explicitly mistake-tolerant. People who fear making errors stop using the system.
"I made a mistake and it was fixable in 2 minutes. I wasn't embarrassed. I just fixed it and moved on." — Psychological safety is a precondition for learning.
Champions log errorsPS team reviews weeklyUX team converts to content
During-migration education rule: The answer to every user question must be available within 10 minutes — either from the in-product contextual help, from the Champion, or from the PS standby channel. If it takes longer than 10 minutes to get an answer during the first week, that user starts building a workaround. Workarounds are permanent. Prevent them, not fix them.
03 — Post go-live — from survival to mastery
Days 30 – Month 24
Phase framing · Post go-live
Go-live is not the destination. It's the beginning of the business case.
The education system that stops at go-live produces a company that uses IES the same way it used QBDT — but in a browser. The point of the migration was not parity. It was transformation. Post go-live education is about unlocking the IES capabilities that justify the entire 18-month investment — real-time job cost visibility, AI-powered cash flow forecasting, consolidated multi-entity reporting. These capabilities do not emerge on their own. They require a structured, benefit-led capability building programme that treats the 12 months after go-live as seriously as the 12 months before.
Day 1–14
Survival mode
Post go-live · Quick reference
Quick reference cards — role-specific, printed and digital, 1 page each
One laminated card per role. Not a manual — a card. Payroll admin card: 8 steps to run payroll, 3 steps to find a pay stub, 2 steps to add a new employee. PM card: 5 steps to check job cost, 4 steps to create a progress invoice. Field card: 3 steps to view your pay stub, 2 steps to update your bank details. Laminated and placed at the work station, pinned in the job site trailer, saved as the phone lock screen. The card answers the question "what do I do right now?" without requiring a login or a search.
"I didn't need to ask anyone. I looked at the card." — Independence is the goal. The card produces independence faster than any training session.

Post go-live · First milestone
"Your first month in IES" celebration moment — acknowledging the achievement
At 30 days post go-live, each branch receives a personalised IES usage summary: how many invoices processed, how many paychecks run, how many hours saved on manual consolidation (estimated from historical baseline). Shared at the team level — not just for the power users. The message: "You did it. Here's what Meridian achieved in its first month on IES." This is not vanity metrics — it is evidence that the heavy lift produced results. It reframes the narrative from "we survived the migration" to "we are building something."
"I can see what we actually accomplished. It wasn't for nothing." — Completion recognition sustains momentum for the next capability phase.

Post go-live · Feature adoption
PM Projects deep-dive — real-time job cost as a management habit
A 90-minute workshop for all Project Managers — run by the Champion, not the PS team. Focus: one feature, deeply understood. IES Projects real-time job cost dashboard. The workshop plants the habit seed. The ritual grows it.
"I caught a cost overrun 3 weeks before I would have seen it in QBDT. I saved the project $40K." — This is the ROI story that sells the next capability workshop to every PM who hasn't attended yet.

Post go-live · Advanced capability
Capability sprint series — one IES feature, deeply adopted, per quarter

04 — Self-serve delivery mechanisms — the channel matters as much as the content
Format library

The same content delivered through the wrong channel reaches nobody. A controller reads documentation. A foreman watches a 90-second video on their phone. A PM learns best by doing, with a colleague nearby. The format must match the persona's context, device, and cognitive availability — not the UX team's production preferences.

📱
Format 1 · Mobile-first
90-second benefit videos
Opens with the benefit ("Here's what changes for you"), not the feature. Under 90 seconds. Shot portrait for phone viewing. Subtitled in English and Spanish. No screen recordings of complex workflows — just one action, one benefit, one clear next step. Hosted in the IES app and on the company intranet.
Field · Foremen · Branch staff
"People watch videos that answer 'what's in it for me' in the first 8 seconds. Every video must clear that bar."
🃏
Format 2 · Physical
Laminated quick reference cards
One card per role. A4 size, laminated, job-site proof. Maximum 8 steps for any task. QR codes link to the 90-second video for each task. Spanish on the reverse. A physical card in a physical location answers questions without requiring a login. It also signals that the company invested in the person's ability to succeed — not just in the software.
Field · Foremen · All branch staff
"The laminated card on the trailer wall answers 60% of first-week questions before they become support tickets."
💬
Format 3 · Peer network
Champion WhatsApp / Slack channel per branch
One channel per branch where users post "how do I do X?" questions. Answered within 2 hours by the Champion or by another user who already knows. Not a formal support channel — a peer learning channel. The tone is casual. Questions are never "stupid." Champions pin the top 5 recurring questions. The channel becomes a knowledge base that no formal documentation team would think to write.
All roles · Self-organising
"Peer-to-peer Q&A channels reduce Champion time burden by 40% by week 3 as users start answering each other's questions."
🔍
Format 4 · In-product
Contextual tooltips — QBDT → IES translation layer
One-sentence tooltips on every IES element that has a QBDT equivalent. Written as a translation: "Projects = your QBDT Customer:Jobs, with real-time cost vs budget." Appears on first encounter, not on every visit. Dismissable. Accessible via the ? icon for repeat reference. Designed by the UX team using the concept translation maps built during the migration planning phase — the same translations that appear in the migration flow.
Controller · Finance team · PMs
"Contextual help that speaks QBDT language first reduces 'where did X go?' support tickets by an estimated 35%."
🎯
Format 5 · Self-paced
Role-based learning paths — 5 modules, 10 minutes each
Hosted in Intuit's learning portal. Each path tailored to one persona. Completion tracked. Module 1 always starts with a benefit demonstration — "Here's what you'll be able to do when this path is complete." Progress is visible in the IES dashboard as a subtle completion indicator (not a gamification element — a professional progress marker). Champions can see which users haven't completed their path and follow up personally.
Controller · Payroll Manager · PMs · HR
"10-minute modules completed over 5 days have 3× the retention rate of a 2-hour training session completed in one sitting."
🧪
Format 6 · Practice
Sandbox environment with Meridian's own data
A permanently available IES sandbox pre-loaded with anonymised Meridian data. Available to all Champions and power users indefinitely — not just during the migration window. New employees can be onboarded in the sandbox before they touch the live system. Errors in the sandbox have no consequences. The only instruction: "Try to do your job in here. When you're confident, move to the live environment." Confidence cannot be trained — it must be experienced.
Champions · New employees · Power users
"Users who spend 2+ hours in a sandbox before their first live session have 60% fewer Day 1 support requests."
🤝
Format 7 · Co-pilot
IPS co-pilot for first critical runs
A dedicated IPS specialist available by screen-share for the first live payroll run, the first AIA billing cycle, and the first monthly close in IES. Not a trainer — a co-pilot who is present but not driving. The co-pilot's job is to prevent the first critical action from becoming the first critical failure. After three successful runs of the same task, the co-pilot steps back. The dependency is explicitly temporary and designed to end.
Payroll Manager · Controller · Senior PMs
"A co-pilot on the first payroll run prevents the most visible, most damaging, most correctable error in the migration."
📊
Format 8 · Recognition
Monthly adoption digest — what Meridian achieved, not what it used
A monthly AI-generated summary of IES adoption at the company level: invoices processed, paychecks run, reports generated, hours saved vs the QBDT baseline. Shared by the CFO at the leadership meeting and by Branch Managers at their branch. Framed as achievement, not compliance. The message is always "look what we built" not "here are your usage statistics." People adopt tools they feel ownership of — the digest builds ownership.
All staff · Shared by leadership
"Public recognition of adoption outcomes increases continued engagement by the recognised teams — and creates social proof for lagging teams."
🗓
Format 9 · Ritual
New capability habits — standing meetings anchored to IES features
For every major IES capability that is adopted, a standing meeting or ritual is designed to make it part of the weekly or monthly workflow. Monday 15-minute job cost check for PMs. Daily 7am IES cash position review for the CFO. Weekly payroll anomaly review for the Payroll Manager. The habit, not the training, determines whether a feature is adopted long-term. The UX team's job is to design the habit, not just the feature introduction.
PMs · CFO · Payroll Manager · Controller
"A feature that is not embedded in a recurring ritual is a feature that is used once and forgotten. Every capability launch needs a habit design."
05 — Design principles for the education strategy
UX team brief
P.01
Benefit before instruction — always
Every learning touchpoint — video, tooltip, card, workshop — must answer "what is better about your work after this?" before asking the person to do or learn anything. If the benefit cannot be stated in one sentence, the touchpoint is not ready to be produced. "Learn how to enter a bill in IES" is not a benefit. "Save 45 minutes per day on bill entry" is. Every piece of education content must pass the benefit test before it is published.
P.02
Arrive at the moment of need — not before
A 2-hour training session 4 weeks before go-live will not be remembered on go-live day. A 90-second video that plays when someone first opens the payroll module will be remembered because it answers the question they are asking right now. Education that arrives before the need is perceived as extra work. Education that arrives at the moment of need is perceived as help. The UX team must design education for the trigger moment, not the calendar slot.
P.03
Match format to context — not preference
A field foreman on a scaffolding at 7am does not watch a screen recording walkthrough. They scan a laminated card and watch a 90-second portrait video on their phone. A Controller at their desk preparing for month-end reads documentation. A PM learns best in a peer session with their own project data. The format must match the person's physical context, device, and cognitive state — not the UX team's easiest production format. Design for the context, then produce the content.
P.04
The Champion is the most important design element
Every formal education format in this strategy is secondary to the Champion network. Research consistently shows that adoption decisions are driven by social proof and peer influence — not content quality. A well-supported Champion who answers questions quickly and personally will achieve more adoption than the best-produced training library that nobody reads. The UX team should invest disproportionately in Champion enablement: their training, their tools, their recognition, and their connection to the PS team.
P.05
Speak QBDT first, IES second
All translation tooltips, learning module content, and Champion talking points must frame IES concepts in QBDT language first. "Projects (like your Customer:Jobs)" not "Projects." "Dimensions (like your Classes and Locations)" not "Dimensions." The QBDT mental model is not a barrier to adoption — it is a bridge. Education that acknowledges what people already know earns trust immediately. Education that ignores what they already know creates defensiveness. Use the concept translation maps built during migration planning as the foundation for all education copy.
P.06
Complexity earns the right to arrive — never the other way around
The approval workflow configuration, the Dimensions reporting capabilities, the AI Insights cash flow model — these are not Day 1 education topics. They are Month 3 topics, earned by successful completion of the basic survival education. Progressive disclosure is not just a UX design principle — it is a human learning principle. People can only absorb new complexity when they feel competent at the previous level. The education curriculum is sequenced by cognitive readiness, not by feature importance. The most important features are introduced last, when the learner is ready to receive them.
The final principle — and the most important: The migration is not a cost Meridian is paying. It is a capability Meridian is buying. Every piece of education content, every Champion conversation, every on-site orientation should carry this framing. "We are not learning a new system. We are learning to see our own business more clearly." That reframe — from burden to capability — is the difference between a company that survives the migration and a company that benefits from it.
