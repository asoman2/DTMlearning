Progress strategy · Dual-axis model · Acceptability-first

Progress is not completion.
Acceptability is progress.

A migration that is 80% technically complete but only 30% accepted by its participants has not migrated at all — it has created a parallel system that nobody trusts. This strategy defines how progress is measured on two independent axes simultaneously, and how divergence between them triggers escalation before it becomes failure.

The two independent axes — both must be tracked, neither can substitute for the other
Axis 1
Technical completion
Have the steps been executed? Data migrated, configurations applied, APIs verified, parallel runs passed. Measurable, objective, binary per step. Easy to track. Easy to over-report.
Axis 2
Participant acceptability
Do the people who depend on this migration believe it is working correctly for them? Behavioural signals, north-star scenario tests, voice of participant, adoption patterns. Harder to measure. Impossible to fake.
The gap between these two axes is the real risk metric. When technical completion races ahead of participant acceptability, the migration is building a system people will work around, not with. The gap is the early warning signal. Catching it at 15% divergence is a conversation. Catching it at 50% divergence is a programme restart.

01 — The divergence matrix — four states a migration can be in
Core model

At any given point in the programme, each wave — and each persona within that wave — sits in one of four states defined by the relationship between technical completion and participant acceptability. The migration dashboard should always show where each wave and each persona sits in this matrix. The worst state is not "low completion, low acceptance" — that is expected early in the programme. The worst state is "high completion, low acceptance" — the migration has happened, but the participants haven't accepted it.

The "running ahead of trust" state is the hidden failure mode in every complex migration. A Programme Manager who only tracks technical completion will see 90% progress and call the migration successful. The Controller who doesn't trust the consolidated P&L, the field supervisors who are still calling HR for pay stubs, the union payroll administrator submitting from a spreadsheet because they don't trust IES — these are invisible in a completion-only tracking system. The dual-axis model makes them visible before they become permanent.
02 — Acceptability defined — per persona, before migration begins
Pre-agreed definitions

Acceptability cannot be measured retrospectively. Each persona's definition of "this migration is working for me" must be agreed, written down, and baselined before the migration begins — ideally in Month 1 of the programme, as part of the Wave 0 setup. These definitions become the north-star scenarios that prove acceptability at each gate. They are not subjective: each definition produces a testable scenario with a pass/fail outcome.

03 — Signal taxonomy — three types of acceptability evidence
How we measure

Acceptability cannot be self-reported alone — people say they have accepted something before they have, especially in professional settings where admitting difficulty feels like failing. The strategy uses three independent signal types that triangulate acceptability from different angles. Only when all three point in the same direction is the acceptability score reliable.

👁
Type 1 · Behavioural
What people do — not what they say
Usage patterns that reveal whether participants have genuinely adopted IES as their working system, or are using it only because they have to while maintaining QBDT as their reference. Behavioural signals are the hardest to fake and the most predictive of long-term adoption.
CFO has not requested the Excel P&L consolidation in 14 days
PM accesses IES Projects 3+ times per week (usage logs)
Field supervisor has not called HR for a pay stub in 7 days
Payroll Manager ran the second payroll without the co-pilot
Controller ran monthly close without opening QBDT to check figures
📊
Type 2 · Data comparison
IES output vs QBDT baseline — objective verification
Systematic comparison of IES outputs against pre-migration QBDT baselines. These are objective, binary, and non-negotiable. A YTD that doesn't match is not an opinion — it's a fact. Data comparison signals are the foundation of financial and payroll acceptability.
IES Balance Sheet = QBDT baseline ± $0 (per entity)
IES YTD per employee = QBDT Payroll Summary ± $0
IES A/R Aging = QBDT A/R Aging (total + individual invoices)
Fringe benefit remittance = CBA schedule ± $0.00 per hour
LCPtracker WH-347 accepted by contracting agency (no reject)
💬
Type 3 · Voice of participant
Structured sentiment at defined moments — not surveys
Not a satisfaction survey. Structured questions asked at specific defined moments by the Champion or PS team. Each question has a qualitative answer that maps to an acceptability signal. The questions are designed to surface the "running ahead of trust" state before it becomes visible in behavioural or data signals.
7-day check-in: "What have you needed to look up in QBDT since go-live?"
14-day check-in: "Is there anything you're doing outside of IES that you expected to do inside it?"
30-day check-in: "If IES went offline for a day, which of your tasks would stop?"
60-day check-in: "Name one thing IES shows you that QBDT didn't."
90-day check-in: "What would need to be true for you to call this migration successful?"
The 30-day voice of participant question is the most diagnostic. "If IES went offline for a day, which of your tasks would stop?" A participant who has genuinely accepted IES names several critical tasks. A participant who hasn't says "I'd switch back to QBDT" or "I'm not sure." This question reveals the degree of dependency — and dependency is the operational definition of acceptance. The Programme Manager should read every answer to this question personally, not receive a summary.
04 — The progress dashboard — visualising both axes simultaneously
Programme Manager view

The key visual treatment: two overlapping bars per row — a wider lighter bar for technical completion, a narrower stronger bar for acceptability. 06 — Divergence escalation — what happens when completion outpaces acceptance
Escalation model

The divergence between technical completion and participant acceptability is measured as a percentage gap. Four thresholds trigger progressively more serious responses. The key design principle: earlier thresholds are responses, not alarms. The programme should not feel like it is in crisis every time a gap appears — gaps are expected, especially in the first 2 weeks of each wave. The escalation model distinguishes between expected divergence (which is managed) and persistent divergence (which is a programme risk).

Divergence ≤10% — expected state
Normal transition — monitor, no action required
Technical completion will always lead acceptability slightly, especially in the first 1–2 weeks of each wave. A gap of 10% or less is healthy: the migration is proceeding at the right pace. The Champion is managing the transition. No escalation. Note in daily digest.

Divergence 10–20% — managed response

Completion is noticeably ahead of acceptance. This is the "running ahead of trust" zone. The Champion for the affected persona group makes personal outreach within 48 hours — not an email, a conversation. The voice of participant question for this window is asked: "What have you needed to look up in QBDT since go-live?" The answer diagnoses the specific gap. Response is targeted to the diagnosed gap.

Divergence 20–35% — PS team intervention

A 20%+ gap means the Champion is not sufficient to close the acceptability deficit. A PS specialist with domain expertise relevant to that persona is assigned — not a general helpdesk resource. The north-star scenario for this persona is scheduled and run within 5 business days. Until it passes, the wave cannot advance to its next stage. Divergence >35% — programme-level escalation
Programme risk — CFO briefed, wave advancement blocked, root cause review within 48 hours
A gap above 35% is a programme risk signal, not just a persona-level adoption issue. It means a fundamental assumption about the migration — whether about readiness, about the IES configuration, or about change management — was wrong. The Programme Manager briefs the CFO within 24 hours. A root cause review (not a post-mortem — this is still in flight) is completed within 48 hours. The review produces three options: pause and remediate, descope the affected persona from this wave, or accept the gap with a written plan to close it within 30 days.

07 — Voice of participant — structured questions at 8 defined moments
Qualitative intelligence

Not surveys. Specific questions, asked at specific moments, by a specific person (the Champion or PS Customer Success Manager), to specific personas. The questions are designed to surface workarounds, hidden QBDT dependency, and confidence gaps before they appear in behavioural data. Each question maps to an acceptability signal in the scoring model. The answers are read by the Programme Manager — not summarised, not aggregated, read.

Moment 1 · Day 3 post go-live
What was the first thing you did in IES today?
Asked by: Champion · Method: In person or phone · Duration: 5 minutes
Healthy answer: A task relevant to their role. Concerning answer: "I logged in to check something, then went back to QBDT to confirm it." Action: If QBDT reference behaviour appears, note it and ask again at Day 7.

08 — Acceptability scoring model — how the number is calculated
Measurement model

The acceptability score for each persona is calculated from a weighted combination of the three signal types. The weights are intentional: behavioural signals carry the most weight because they are the hardest to game and the most predictive of long-term adoption. Data comparison signals are binary and non-negotiable — a financial baseline that doesn't tie cannot be "mostly acceptable." Voice of participant is weighted last because self-reporting is the least reliable predictor of actual adoption.

Structured qualitative		 15% Each voice of participant question scores 1 (healthy), 0.5 (neutral or uncertain), or 0 (concerning/QBDT reference). The data comparison weight is intentionally asymmetric in one case: For the Payroll Manager, Certified Payroll Officer, and Union Stewards, any data comparison score of 0 (unresolved mismatch) sets the overall acceptability score to 0 regardless of behavioural and voice signals. A payroll administrator who loves using IES but has an unresolved YTD discrepancy is not acceptable — the discrepancy is a compliance risk. Financial and payroll accuracy is the non-negotiable floor. Everything else is measured above that floor.
09 — Integration with the migration flow
Flow design requirements

The progress tracking and acceptability strategy is not a separate dashboard bolted onto the migration flow — it is embedded in the flow itself. Every step card in the flow contributes to the dual-axis model. Every gate convergence card shows acceptability thresholds alongside technical completion thresholds. The Programme Manager's Layer 1 view is the dual-axis dashboard.
