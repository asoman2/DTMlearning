01 — Company profile — Meridian Building Group
Assumed profile

Meridian Building Group is a commercial general contractor headquartered in Dallas, TX. It operates branch offices in 10 states, each functioning as a semi-autonomous profit centre with its own payroll, project portfolio, and in some cases its own legal entity. The company has grown through acquisition — meaning branch offices may have different CoA structures, QBDT versions, chart configurations, and accounting methods. This is the norm, not the exception, for companies at this scale.

Headquarters
Dallas, TX (primary entity)
Parent company QBDT file · ~80 employees · Holds corporate overhead, equipment fleet, and intercompany transactions. Primary EIN. Full payroll and AP/AR operations.
Branch states
CA, FL, NY, CO, AZ, GA, NC, OH, WA
Each branch has 10–20 field + office employees, its own QBDT file (or shared with HQ), state-specific payroll registrations, workers' comp carriers, and possibly a separate legal entity / EIN.
Workforce composition
~210 employees across 4 workforce types
Salaried staff: PMs, estimators, admin (55) · Hourly non-union: field crews in TX, AZ, GA, NC (85) · Union: NY, CA, WA, OH, CO (50) · 1099 subcontractors: ~120 active subs on any given month · Prevailing wage applies on ~40% of project portfolio.
Current tech stack
QBDT Enterprise 2022 (10 files) + patchwork integrations
Procore (project management) · LCPtracker (certified payroll, federal jobs) · Viewpoint Vista (legacy, being sunset) · MS Excel (job cost consolidation — manually updated weekly) · ADP (used by 3 branches before Meridian acquisition) · Custom SQL reporting on HQ file.
Revenue composition
$75M · 3 contract types
Federal / public works (38%): Davis-Bacon Act prevailing wage, certified payroll, AIA G702/G703 billing · Commercial private (52%): standard AIA billing, retainage, lien management · T&M and service (10%): time-and-material, monthly billing
Migration complicating factors
5 factors that make this non-standard
Multiple EINs: 3 legal entities across 10 states · Acquired branches: inconsistent CoA and payroll item naming · Active federal contracts: certified payroll cannot lapse · Union agreements: NY, CA, WA collective bargaining agreements govern payroll changes · Retainage: $4.2M held across 23 active projects

04 — Migration phases — realistic 18-month programme
Staggered by state

Meridian cannot go live in all 10 states on the same day. A staggered rollout — HQ first, then the least-complex states, then union/federal states last — reduces risk, allows lessons from early states to inform later ones, and ensures that if something goes wrong, it affects one branch rather than ten simultaneously.

Recommended go-live sequence: Wave 1: TX (HQ), AZ, GA, NC — non-union, lower complexity. Wave 2: FL, CO, OH — moderate complexity. Wave 3: CA, NY, WA — union, prevailing wage, certified payroll, highest compliance risk. Each wave is ~8 weeks apart. Wave 3 begins only after Wave 1 has completed 30 days of stable operation.

06 — Parallel run strategy — what it actually means at this scale
Per-wave

A parallel run at a 10-state construction company is not running two software systems simultaneously. It is running two financial record-keeping systems for the same period of time, comparing their outputs nightly, and making a deliberate decision about when each branch is stable enough to decommission QBDT. The parallel run per wave runs for a minimum of 5 business nights. Union payroll branches require a full pay period parallel (2 weeks minimum) before decommission.

What gets compared every night
Check and payroll register: QBDT vs IES — every employee, every deduction line
Payroll tax liability balances: federal 941 deposits, state withholding, SUI — per state
A/R aging: open invoices and AIA applications — total balance and individual items
A/P aging: open bills, subcontractor invoices, equipment lease payments
WIP schedule: cost to date per active project vs QBDT job cost report
Bank register: daily transaction count and ending balance per account
Union fringe benefit accruals: CA, NY, WA, OH, CO — per CBA schedule
What decommission is gated on
5 consecutive clean nights: all registers match, no unexplained variances
First live payroll run completed without issues — direct deposits confirmed
At least one AIA application created and approved in IES by a PM in this wave
Controller sign-off on monthly close in IES (for that branch)
CPA confirmation: opening balances accurate for this entity
Wave 3 only: CPO sign-off on one clean LCPtracker submission from IES data
Wave 3 only: Union steward review of first union payroll output in IES
Likely parallel run exceptions (plan for these)
OH municipal tax: first pay period will almost certainly show per-city discrepancies as work location data from QB Time is validated
CA SDI/ETT: rounding method differences between QBDT and IES — expect $1–$5 differences per employee, all explainable
Union fringes: hour-based fringe calculations may differ from QBDT's flat-rate approach — requires explicit reconciliation against the CBA schedule
WA L&I: no IES equivalent — parallel run will always show a difference for WA. This is expected and must be documented as a known gap, not a failure.
Retainage: IES and QBDT will show retainage in different CoA locations — the total must match, the location will differ. Ensure the comparison is on total, not account-by-account.
Who reviews the morning digest
Controller: A/R, A/P, WIP, and Balance Sheet variances
Payroll Manager: payroll register and tax liability variances
Certified Payroll Officer (Wave 3): LCPtracker submission status
Branch Manager: local bank register and any local vendor escalations
IPS team: overnight exception queue — each unexplained item has a root cause and resolution option by 9am
Programme Manager: overall dashboard — red/amber/green per branch per night

08 — Process changes & IES feature adoption — the transformation layer
Months 14–24

Migration is the floor, not the ceiling. A company that has successfully moved to IES but is still using Excel for consolidated reporting, email for expense approvals, and phone calls for subcontractor payment status has not realised the value of the platform. The following process changes represent what a fully utilised IES looks like for Meridian — and the education required to get there.

CFO & Executive team
From: Weekly Excel P&L · To: IES Live Multi-Entity Dashboard
The CFO currently receives a manually assembled Excel report every Monday morning. Post-migration, the IES multi-entity dashboard provides real-time consolidated P&L with branch-level and job-level drill-down. Education required: 3-hour executive dashboard workshop. IES AI Insights provides cash flow forecasting and variance alerts. This is not a configuration task — it is a mindset shift from periodic reporting to continuous visibility.
Project Managers
From: Monthly job cost reports · To: Real-time IES Projects dashboard
PMs currently see job cost actuals once a month when the Controller closes the books. In IES, cost-to-date is visible in real time as subcontractor bills are approved and time entries are submitted. Education required: 4-week IES Projects certification programme for all 15–20 PMs. The change management challenge: PMs who have worked with monthly reporting for years will not naturally check a daily dashboard. New habits require new meeting structures (weekly job cost review cadence).
Controller & Finance team
From: Manual month-end close · To: Continuous close with approval workflows
IES approval workflows mean invoices are reviewed and approved in the system — not via email. Recurring transactions auto-post. Bank feeds are reconciled daily rather than monthly. The Controller's month-end close should drop from 10 days to 5 days within 6 months of full adoption. Education required: 2-day IES Finance Essentials workshop + 30-day coaching on approval workflow adoption.
Field supervisors & crews
From: Paper timesheets / phone call payroll queries · To: QB Time + IES self-service
Field workers currently submit paper timesheets or call the office to report hours. QB Time mobile app allows real-time GPS-tagged time entry by job and cost code. IES self-service gives employees access to their own pay stubs, W-2s, and benefits information without calling HR. Education required: 30-minute on-site QB Time orientation per job site, bilingual, before first Wave go-live. Plan for a 3-month adoption curve — paper timesheets will persist in parallel until supervisors are confident.
Payroll & HR
From: Multi-system payroll administration · To: Single IES payroll with AI monitoring
Currently, Corporate Payroll runs 10 separate QBDT payroll files with manual consolidation. Post-migration, all 210 employees run through a single IES payroll. The Payroll Manager's workload should reduce by ~30% within 12 months of full adoption — but only after the IES payroll configuration is stable and certified payroll, union fringes, and multi-state tax are all working correctly. The first 6 months post go-live are higher workload than QBDT, not lower.
What IES cannot replace (honest)
IES is not an ERP. Do not try to make it one.
IES does not replace Procore for project management. It does not replace LCPtracker for certified payroll. It does not replace a fleet management tool. It does not replace a BI tool for complex consolidated reporting. The right post-migration architecture is IES as the financial backbone, connected to best-of-breed tools for construction operations. Attempting to force all operations into IES will result in a worse outcome than the current QBDT + patchwork stack. The CFO needs to accept this architecture decision explicitly.
The realistic 24-month outcome: Meridian Building Group on a fully adopted IES — with Procore, QB Time, LCPtracker, Levelset, and a BI dashboard connected — has real-time job cost visibility across all 10 states, eliminates the weekly Excel consolidation, reduces the month-end close from 10 days to 4–5 days, automates subcontractor COI and lien waiver workflows, and gives field workers self-service access to their own pay information. The CFO gets a daily view of cash position and project profitability that was previously unavailable. That outcome is achievable — but it requires the full 18–24 months, the full team engagement, and an honest relationship with the gaps described above.
