Critical workflow analysis — QBDT Enterprise → IES

We are

QuickBooks Desktop Enterprise is not a data store — it is a deeply configured operational system. Inventory assemblies, class tracking, job costing, custom fields, industry workflows built over years. Moving that to IES and calling it "migration" is technically accurate and experientially wrong. This document names the real gaps, then proposes a collaborative pipeline that can actually close them.

01 — The critical workflow gaps

These are not missing features that will ship in Q3. They are structural differences in how QBDT Enterprise and IES model business operations. Telling a customer their workflows will "migrate" when these gaps exist is the source of post-migration churn, escalations, and lost trust.

Gap 01 · Inventory
Advanced inventory depth and assembly manufacturing
QBDT Enterprise has serial/lot number tracking, bin location management, FIFO costing, barcode scanning, multiple warehouse locations, and build assembly workflows — including work-in-progress tracking, component shortfall alerts, and unit cost rollups. IES has strong inventory fundamentals but the manufacturing and warehouse management depth simply does not have a 1:1 equivalent for a distributor or light manufacturer running on QBDT Enterprise today.
Business impact if not addressed
Companies using QBDT Enterprise for inventory management will need a separate WMS or manufacturing app post-migration — an unplanned integration that was never part of the migration conversation.
Gap 02 · Conceptual translation
Projects and dimensions are not natural language for a QBDT user
IES uses Projects and Dimensions (Classes + Locations unified) as its primary segment reporting model. QBDT Enterprise users think in terms of Jobs, Classes, Customer:Job hierarchies, and Service Items. These are not the same concepts. A contractor who has built eight years of job cost reporting on the QBDT class structure will not recognise their data in IES unless someone explicitly maps each concept and demonstrates that the reporting they depend on still works.
Business impact if not addressed
Finance and operations teams arrive in IES not understanding why their segment reports look different. They distrust the data, revert to QBDT for reconciliation, and delay the transition indefinitely.
Gap 03 · Job costing
Job cost reporting doesn't have a direct migration path
QBDT Enterprise job costing — estimated vs actual costs, job cost detail by phase, profitability by job, WIP reporting — is one of the primary reasons construction and professional services companies choose QBDT Enterprise over standard QBO. IES's Projects feature can replicate many of these capabilities, but the migration engine does not automatically translate QBDT Job cost structures into IES Projects with the correct budget and actuals mapping.
Business impact if not addressed
Construction and professional services companies — the largest QBDT Enterprise segment — lose their primary reason for upgrading. They land in IES and immediately ask "where are my job cost reports?"
Gap 04 · Custom fields and forms
Custom fields, memorised reports, and form templates are orphaned
QBDT Enterprise supports extensive custom field configurations at the customer, vendor, item, and transaction level — often built by the company's accountant or IT team over years. Memorised report groups are another operational dependency. Neither custom fields nor memorised reports have an automated migration path to IES. They are silently abandoned.
Business impact if not addressed
Operational teams who depended on custom field data (customer category codes, vendor compliance flags, item tracking attributes) discover their data is missing weeks after go-live — after the QBDT file has been archived.
Gap 05 · Pricing and price levels
Price levels and per-customer pricing rules don't transfer
QBDT Enterprise supports multiple price levels per item, per-customer price level assignments, and quantity-break pricing. A distributor with 200 customers on differentiated pricing structures has those rules embedded in QBDT. IES has price rules capabilities but they are structured differently — and the migration engine moves the items, not the pricing matrix.
Business impact if not addressed
Sales team issues the first invoice post-migration and quotes the wrong price to a preferred customer. That error reaches the customer before anyone realises the pricing rules didn't migrate.
Gap 06 · Audit and control
Closing date enforcement and audit trail parity gaps
QBDT Enterprise's closing date password, audit trail, and transaction lock mechanisms behave differently from IES's period close and permission model. Companies in regulated industries, or those undergoing audits, expect the same hard-close guarantees. The role-based permission model in IES is actually stronger — but the transition between paradigms is not explained or managed during migration.
Business impact if not addressed
Accountants and controllers discover post-migration that their close-date controls work differently than expected. In an audit context, this gap is a compliance risk, not just a UX inconvenience.

The honest statement: IES is a more capable platform than QBDT Enterprise in most dimensions. The problem is not what IES can do — it is the absence of a translation layer that connects what the customer knows how to do in QBDT to how it is done in IES. Right now that translation lives in the customer's head, or in their accountant's experience, or it doesn't happen at all.

02 — Conceptual translation map — QBDT → IES

Before any configuration begins, every business must understand what their QBDT concepts become in IES. This is not a feature comparison. It is a language translation. The concepts below are the ones that consistently disorient migrating customers.

QBDT Enterprise concept IES equivalent Parity What the customer must understand — and what is genuinely different
Classes
Segment tagging on transactions		 Dimensions (Classes + Locations) ✓ Parity IES unifies Classes and Locations into a single Dimensions model. More powerful — but a customer who had separate Class and Location tracking will need a mapping decision: do Classes become one Dimension and Locations another, or are they merged? This decision must be made before migration, not discovered after.
Customer:Job hierarchy
Parent customer with sub-jobs		 Projects (under a Customer) ~ Partial IES Projects are more capable than QBDT Jobs — they support budget vs actual tracking, profitability reporting, and time integration. But QBDT's multi-level Customer:Job hierarchy (customer → job → phase → cost code) does not map cleanly to IES Projects' flat structure. Deep hierarchies require a deliberate re-architecting, not a direct migration.
Items (Inventory, Non-inventory, Service, Assembly) Products & Services (with inventory tracking) ~ Partial Standard items migrate well. Assembly items (Bill of Materials) have no direct IES equivalent without a manufacturing integration. Inventory Assembly items that track builds and component consumption will either need a third-party manufacturing app or a significant workflow redesign in IES.
Price Levels
Per-customer or per-item pricing rules		 Price Rules / Custom Pricing ~ Partial IES supports custom pricing rules but structures them differently from QBDT's price levels. A flat percentage discount level in QBDT must be re-created as an explicit price rule in IES. Quantity-break pricing requires a manual rule creation for each break point. The migration engine does not carry pricing rules — only item list prices.
Custom Fields (on customers, vendors, items, transactions) Custom Fields (limited to specific record types) ✗ Gap IES has custom fields but at a different level of granularity than QBDT Enterprise. Some QBDT custom field placements have no IES equivalent location. The data in those fields does not migrate — it must be manually mapped to an available field, or that data category is permanently lost. This gap is invisible until someone looks for the data post-migration.
Memorised Reports and Report Groups Custom Reports (must be rebuilt) ✗ Gap No migration path exists for QBDT memorised reports. A finance team that opens IES on Day 1 and tries to run their standard morning reports will find none of them. This is predictable, preventable, and consistently surprises customers because it is never surfaced during the migration conversation.
Serial / Lot Number Tracking Not available natively in IES ✗ Gap Serial and lot tracking requires a third-party app integration (e.g. Katana, SOS Inventory, Fishbowl). If this is a core operational dependency, it must be scoped as an integration requirement and tested before go-live — not discovered post-migration when the first serialised inventory receipt fails.
Multiple Warehouse / Bin Locations Partial — Locations dimension, no bin-level ✗ Gap IES supports multiple inventory locations at the warehouse level but does not support bin-level tracking natively. Businesses with bin-level inventory control — distribution centres, manufacturing operations — need a WMS integration. This is not communicated during the migration sales process.
Closing Date Password
Hard-close enforcement mechanism		 Period Close + Role-based permissions ~ Different IES's period close model is more sophisticated but works differently. The QBDT "closing date password" is a single hard lock. IES uses role-based permissions per period. The outcome is similar — prior-period protection — but the mechanism is different enough that accountants who depend on closing date enforcement need explicit orientation before go-live.
03 — The collaborative migration pipeline
Proposed approach
From data transport to operational transformation
The migration experience needs to be rebuilt around a single honest premise: the customer is not migrating a file — they are re-platforming an operating system. That requires a structured engagement model where AI does the diagnostic and translation work, Professional Services provides industry expertise and configuration guidance, and the customer's own stakeholders — finance, operations, warehouse, HR — are brought in at the moments their domain knowledge is actually needed. Not as spectators to a technical process, but as co-designers of their own IES environment.
01
Stage 1 · Intake & AI Discovery
Deep diagnostic — AI reads the operational DNA of the QBDT file
Before any human reviews anything, AI performs a comprehensive analysis of the QBDT Enterprise file — not just the data, but the operational patterns. Which custom fields are populated by more than 80% of records (operationally critical)? Which classes map to departments vs job types vs segments? What is the Customer:Job hierarchy depth? Which items have assemblies? What percentage of inventory uses serial or lot numbers? This diagnostic produces an Operational DNA report — a plain-English description of how this business actually runs — before a single migration configuration question is asked.
AI automated PS team receives
Industry detection: AI classifies the business by operational pattern — not just by NAICS code. A "construction" company running primarily service workflows is treated differently from one running WIP-heavy project management.
Complexity scoring: Inventory complexity, reporting complexity, user configuration depth, and integration dependency each scored independently. The score drives which pipeline tier this customer lands in — standard guided, custom configured, or full Professional Services engagement.
Gap pre-detection: AI flags known-gap categories before the customer is onboarded — serial/lot tracking, assembly manufacturing, deep job cost hierarchy, custom field proliferation. Customer hears about these gaps from Intuit first, not discovers them post-migration.
Stakeholder map: Based on the operational DNA, AI recommends which internal roles must be engaged at each pipeline stage: "Your file shows deep job cost tracking — your Project Manager or Site Supervisor needs to be part of the Projects configuration session."
02
Stage 2 · AI Translation Intelligence
Conceptual translation — map every QBDT construct to its IES equivalent, explicitly
AI generates a draft Concept Translation Map for this specific customer — not a generic table, but a document that shows exactly how this customer's classes, jobs, price levels, custom fields, and item types map to their IES equivalents. Where the mapping is clean, it is confirmed. Where there is a gap or a structural difference, AI surfaces three design options and explains the trade-offs in business language — not technical language. The PS team reviews and annotates before the customer ever sees it.
AI generates PS team annotates Accountant reviews
Dimension design session: AI produces a proposed Dimensions structure for IES based on the customer's class/location usage patterns. Customer's accountant confirms or adjusts. No dimension decision is made without a human with business context in the room.
Projects architecture: For job-costing-heavy customers, AI proposes a Project hierarchy design — which QBDT Customer:Jobs become IES Projects, which become sub-projects, and how phases and cost codes are modelled. Presented to the Project Manager, not the accountant.
Gap decision log: For each identified gap (serial tracking, assembly builds, custom field locations), AI generates a decision prompt: "Your business uses lot tracking for [X items]. Here are three ways to handle this in IES: [A] third-party WMS, [B] manual lot notes field, [C] defer to post-migration. Which fits your operation?" Each decision is logged, attributed, and used to configure the migration scope.
Report re-creation plan: AI analyses the customer's top 10 most-run QBDT reports (from usage data) and drafts the IES equivalent configuration for each. Finance team confirms. No customer arrives on Day 1 without their key reports already built.
03
Stage 3 · Collaborative Co-Design
Stakeholder workshops — configure IES to match the business, not the default template
This is where the collaborative model differs most sharply from the current experience. Instead of the PS team configuring IES and presenting it to the customer, the configuration happens in sessions where the right domain expert is in the room. Inventory sessions include the warehouse manager. Project configuration sessions include the job site supervisor. Approval workflow sessions include the controller. Each session is pre-loaded by AI with the draft configuration from Stage 2 — the human session is for validation, exception handling, and decisions that require judgment.
PS team facilitates Business stakeholders Accountant AI captures decisions
Finance configuration session: Chart of Accounts sign-off, Dimensions design confirmation, approval workflow thresholds, period close permissions. Attendees: Controller, external accountant. Duration: 90 minutes with pre-work delivered by AI the day before.
Inventory and operations session: Item migration review, pricing rule recreation, inventory location mapping, gap decisions (serial/lot, assemblies). Attendees: Warehouse manager, purchasing manager, operations lead.
Project and job cost session: Customer:Job → Projects architecture, budget template creation, cost code mapping, profitability report rebuilding. Attendees: Project manager, job site supervisors, estimator.
Payroll and HR session: (Where applicable) Employee record review, YTD confirmation, deduction mapping, pay stub format orientation, Pay History walkthrough. Attendees: HR manager, payroll processor.
Decision capture: AI records every configuration decision made in each session — who decided what, when, and why. This becomes the Configuration Rationale Document — the single source of truth if a decision needs to be revisited post-go-live.
04
Stage 4 · AI-Assisted Configuration Build
Automated IES configuration — from decisions to a working environment
With the co-design decisions captured, AI builds the IES environment to match the agreed configuration. Chart of Accounts structure, Dimensions hierarchy, Project templates, pricing rules, approval workflows, custom fields, user roles, and report configurations are all applied programmatically from the decision log. The PS team reviews the output. The customer gets a configured IES environment — not a blank platform with default settings — before data migration begins.
AI builds PS team reviews
Industry template as baseline: IES environment is initialised with an industry-specific template (construction, distribution, professional services, manufacturing, non-profit) rather than a blank default. Industry-appropriate Chart of Accounts, report library, approval workflow patterns, and dimension structures are pre-loaded.
AI Insights seeding: IES's AI Insights is pre-configured with the KPIs most relevant to this industry — job cost variance for contractors, inventory turnover for distributors, utilisation rate for professional services. The customer opens IES on Day 1 and their dashboard already speaks their language.
Integration pipeline assembly: For gap items (serial tracking, WMS, manufacturing app), integration connections are established, configured, and tested during Stage 4 — not discovered as missing after go-live.
Data migration runs: With IES fully configured, the data migration engine runs against a prepared target environment — not a blank one. The paycheck migration, CoA migration, and list migrations run into an environment that already knows what to do with the data.
05
Stage 5 · Stakeholder Validation
Role-by-role sign-off — every team confirms their workflows work
Validation is not a single UAT session — it is a structured role-by-role confirmation where each stakeholder who participated in Stage 3 tests the specific workflows they depend on. The warehouse manager tests an inventory receipt and a build. The project manager runs a job cost report. The controller runs a trial balance and confirms the period close works. AI monitors the validation sessions and flags any discrepancy between what was decided in Stage 3 and what the environment produces.
All business stakeholders PS team observes AI monitors
North-star scenario testing per role: Each stakeholder has a pre-defined primary scenario — the one task that, if it works correctly, proves their domain is migrated successfully. For the warehouse manager: receive a serialised item. For the project manager: run an estimated vs actual cost report for an in-progress job.
AI discrepancy detection: AI compares validation outputs against the Configuration Rationale Document decisions. "The job cost report you are running shows only labour costs — but in your Stage 3 session, you specified it should also include materials allocated to the job. This appears to be a configuration gap."
Formal go/no-go per domain: Each domain (finance, operations, payroll, projects) has a named sign-off. The go-live decision is not made at the project level — it is made when every domain lead has signed off on their workflows. One domain not ready blocks go-live for that domain only — not for the whole business.
06
Stage 6 · Go-Live + Continuous AI Intelligence
Go-live is the beginning of the relationship — not the end of the engagement
The current migration experience ends at go-live. The proposed pipeline continues. AI monitors IES usage patterns for 90 days post-go-live and surfaces signals: which features are being underused relative to the customer's industry peers, which workflows are producing exceptions at a higher rate than expected, which IES capabilities — Projects, Dimensions, AI Insights — the team has not yet adopted. The goal is to ensure that the customer actually realises the IES capabilities they paid for, not just that their QBDT data is in a new location.
AI continuous monitor PS team on standby Customer team
Adoption signals: "Your team has been running payroll correctly for 30 days — well done. But your Projects feature has not been used yet. Based on your job cost history in QBDT, this feature would give you real-time profitability visibility per project. Here is a 10-minute setup guide for your Project Manager."
AI Insights benchmarking: 90 days post-migration, IES has enough data to run industry benchmarks. "Your gross margin on construction projects is 18% — the median for your industry segment in your region is 24%. Here are the three job cost line items where your projects consistently run over budget."
Capability expansion prompts: As the customer stabilises, AI surfaces the next level of IES capability relevant to their industry — Workforce management for a contractor, AP automation for a distributor, revenue recognition for a professional services firm. Migration becomes the entry point for ongoing value realisation.
Churn early-warning: Specific usage drops — Pay History not accessed, Projects not updated, Dimensions not being applied to transactions — trigger a PS team alert before the customer contacts support or considers reverting.
04 — Industry-specific migration modules

The pipeline is not a single template applied to every customer. It is a configurable framework with industry modules that pre-load the right conceptual translation, gap handling, and IES configuration for each business type. The AI diagnostic in Stage 1 routes the customer to the correct module automatically.

🏗
Construction & Contracting
Job cost hierarchy, WIP, subcontractor management
Critical QBDT dependency
Customer:Job hierarchy with phases and cost codes; estimated vs actual job cost reports; subcontractor certificate of insurance tracking; AIA billing formats; WIP schedules.
IES capability
Projects with budget templates, custom cost categories, Progress Invoicing, time & expense tracking per project. Stronger than QBDT for active project visibility.
Pipeline translation required
Every QBDT job becomes an IES Project. Cost code structure maps to custom Project cost categories. Phase billing maps to Progress Invoicing milestones. This cannot be automated — it requires a 90-minute session with the estimator and project manager.
📦
Wholesale Distribution
Multi-location inventory, price levels, purchase orders
Critical QBDT dependency
Multiple warehouse locations; per-customer price levels; quantity break pricing; bin management; serial/lot tracking; drop shipment workflows; vendor lead times.
IES capability
Multi-location inventory at warehouse level, custom pricing rules, advanced PO management, 850+ app integrations for WMS. Better reporting than QBDT on inventory performance.
Pipeline translation required
Warehouse locations map to IES Locations dimension. Price levels must be manually rebuilt as Price Rules. Serial/lot tracking requires a WMS integration decision made in Stage 2 — before migration scope is set. Bin management is a confirmed gap requiring a third-party app.
⚙
Light Manufacturing
Assembly builds, BOM, production orders, WIP
Critical QBDT dependency
Assembly items with Bills of Materials; build/pending build orders; component shortfall alerts; WIP inventory valuation; FIFO costing; production cost tracking.
IES capability
IES does not have native BOM or production order management. This is the largest structural gap in the QBDT Enterprise → IES migration for manufacturers.
Pipeline translation required
Stage 1 AI diagnostic must detect assembly item usage and immediately surface the manufacturing gap. Stage 2 must present a manufacturing integration decision (Katana, SOS, Fishbowl) with clear scope and cost implications. Manufacturing customers without this conversation should not be migrated without an agreed integration plan.
💼
Professional Services
Time tracking, project billing, utilisation, revenue recognition
Critical QBDT dependency
Time and expense tracking by client and project; billable vs non-billable hours; client profitability reporting; invoice creation from approved time; retainer management.
IES capability
Projects with time tracking, billable expense management, client profitability dashboards, revenue recognition workflows. This is arguably IES's strongest segment vs QBDT — significantly more capable.
Pipeline translation required
The opportunity gap: professional services firms that fully adopt IES Projects get better client visibility than QBDT ever offered. The pipeline session must reframe this as a capability upgrade — not a translation — and bring the engagement manager into the configuration session.
🏥
Non-profit & Healthcare-adjacent
Fund accounting, grant tracking, donor restrictions, compliance
Critical QBDT dependency
Class-based fund tracking; restricted vs unrestricted net assets; grant expense tracking; programme allocation; donor reporting; Form 990 preparation support.
IES capability
Dimensions for fund and programme tracking, project-based grant management, custom financial statement templates. Not a native fund accounting system — but capable with correct configuration.
Pipeline translation required
Each restricted fund maps to a Dimension value. Grant tracking moves to Projects. The Stage 3 session must include the development director, not just the accountant — they own the donor reporting requirements. Form 990 preparation still requires external support or a specialised integration.
05 — Design principles for the pipeline
P.01
Name the gaps before the customer discovers them
Every known feature parity gap — serial tracking, assembly manufacturing, bin management, memorised reports — must be surfaced by the AI diagnostic in Stage 1, before any commitment is made. A customer who hears about a gap from Intuit before migration has a choice. A customer who discovers it post-migration has a grievance.
P.02
Route each decision to the person with the relevant knowledge
The warehouse manager knows how their inventory is organised. The project manager knows how jobs are structured. The controller knows how periods are closed. Configuration decisions made without the right domain expert produce configurations that are technically valid but operationally wrong. The pipeline routes each decision to the person who should make it.
P.03
AI does the knowing. Humans make the judgment calls.
AI is faster and more thorough than any PS team at reading a QBDT file, classifying its operational patterns, and generating configuration drafts. But AI cannot replace the judgment of a warehouse manager who knows that "Warehouse A" is actually a truck, not a building. The pipeline is designed so AI exhausts what it can automate, and humans only see decisions that require judgment.
P.04
Industry-specific language, not platform-generic language
A contractor should never hear the word "Dimensions" in their migration. They should hear "how do you want to track costs per job phase?" A distributor should never hear "Projects" — they should hear "do you want to track profitability per customer account?" The translation layer is not just technical — it is linguistic. IES features must be explained in the vocabulary of the customer's industry.
P.05
Go-live is an entry point, not a finish line
The current migration experience treats go-live as delivery. The proposed pipeline treats go-live as the beginning of a 90-day IES adoption period. The most valuable IES capabilities — Projects, AI Insights, Dimensions-based reporting, approval workflows — are not used on Day 1. They are adopted gradually as the team builds confidence. The pipeline is designed to accelerate that adoption through continuous AI-guided nudges.
P.06
The migration scope adapts to the business — not vice versa
A light manufacturer with complex assembly workflows and a professional services firm with simple time billing are not the same migration. The pipeline's industry module system means the scope, session structure, integration requirements, and success criteria are customised per customer type in Stage 1. No customer is forced through a workflow designed for a different business model.
The reframe
Stop selling a migration.
Start selling an operational upgrade.
The customers who get the most from IES are not the ones who migrated their QBDT data. They are the ones who understood that IES is a fundamentally different kind of business operating system — one that offers real-time project profitability, AI-generated cash flow insights, collaborative approval workflows, and industry benchmarking — and who then rebuilt their operational model to take advantage of it.
The gap is not that IES lacks features. The gap is that the migration experience does not create the understanding required to use those features. A contractor who migrates and never learns about Projects will run IES like a worse version of QBDT. A contractor who understands that Projects is a real-time job cost monitor — not a data container — will make decisions faster, catch cost overruns earlier, and bill more accurately.
The collaborative pipeline proposed here is the mechanism for creating that understanding at scale — using AI to do the diagnostic and translation work that no PS team can afford to do manually for every customer, and using structured stakeholder sessions to ensure that the people who will actually use IES are the ones who configure it.
