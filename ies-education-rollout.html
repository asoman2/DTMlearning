01 — Five workforce tiers — IES relationship, not org chart position
Segmentation model

The segmentation that matters for education design is not the org chart — it is each person's actual relationship with IES. A Controller and a field laborer are not in adjacent tiers of an org chart, but they are in adjacent tiers of IES relationship (both are deeply affected; one configures, one has their paycheck generated). The five tiers cut across departments and seniority levels.

Tier 5 is the most overlooked education population in every migration. A field worker who shows up on a Monday after go-live and gets paid correctly is a silent success. One who gets paid incorrectly — wrong hours, wrong deductions, missing union fringe benefit — calls their foreman and the foreman calls the branch manager and the branch manager calls corporate. The entire visible symptom of a Tier 5 education failure is a payroll complaint chain. Prevent it with a 10-minute on-site conversation and a card.
02 — Discover → Understand → Confirm — adapted for each tier
Universal architecture

The cognitive sequence is identical across all five tiers. What changes is the subject matter, the channel, the time investment, and the confirmation format. A Tier 1 Controller confirming the WIP schedule automation is in the same DSU sequence as a Tier 5 field worker confirming their pay stub shows the right deductions. Same three steps. Completely different content, depth, and context.

The Tier 4 DSU is the highest-volume education event in the programme. ~115 employees need to know one thing: their pay stub is on their phone. The DSU for this group is compressed to 15 minutes, conducted by the Champion or foreman, using the employee's own device and their own actual pay stub. The "context" in "understand in context" is literally their paycheck number — not an abstract example. This is the most powerful possible personalisation: showing someone their own data before asking them to do anything with it.
03 — Trigger moments — education arrives when it is needed, not before
Just-in-time delivery

The most important principle in the rollout is not what to teach — it is when. Education delivered before the moment of need is perceived as extra work. Education delivered at the moment of need is perceived as help. Every education touchpoint is anchored to a specific trigger event in the migration programme, not to a calendar date or a content schedule.

04 — IES-ambient education — for people who never log in
Tier 5 strategy

For Tier 5 employees — those who will never have an IES login — the education is not about software. It is about reassurance and orientation: their money is safe, the process that generates their paycheck has changed, and here is the one person they can call if something looks wrong. This is a human communication strategy, not a technology education strategy. The UX team's role is to design the foreman's talking points and the printed card — not an app or a portal.

💬
Format 1 · Oral delivery
Foreman delivers the message on-site
The foreman receives a 3-point script from the Champion — not a long document, three sentences. (1) "We switched to a new payroll system last week." (2) "Your money arrives the same way, same day, same amount." (3) "If your paycheck looks different, call me first — here's what's normal and here's what isn't." The foreman is the trusted source, not HR. This is by design.
🃏
Format 2 · Printed card
One card, two sides, two languages
A wallet-sized or trailer-wall card with one paycheck illustration on each side: QBDT stub on the left, IES stub on the right. Three differences highlighted: stub format, company name in the header (may change for entities), deduction label changes. And at the bottom: Champion's first name and phone number. Not the HR department. Not a ticket system. A name and a number. This card goes home with the worker after the foreman session.
🤝
Format 3 · Union steward co-sign
For Wave 3: steward delivers the fringe benefit message
For union workers in CA, NY, WA, OH, CO — the message about fringe benefit calculation changes must come from the steward, not from Meridian management. The steward reviews the new fringe benefit configuration before go-live, confirms it matches the CBA, and then delivers the "here's what's changing in your benefit fund contributions" message to their members. Management delivers system changes. Steward delivers benefit changes. This distinction matters to union members.
📞
Format 4 · Hot line — not a ticket
One phone number, answered by a person, first week only
For the first week after each wave go-live, the IES Champion (or a designated HR admin) is on call for Tier 5 payroll questions. Not a ticket. Not "email HR." A phone number that rings a person. This is expensive for one week and nearly free every other week — because after the first payroll is processed correctly, Tier 5 questions drop to nearly zero. The first paycheck is the product. Everything else is support for the product.
📄
Format 5 · Stub comparison sheet
Old stub format vs new — annotated, printed
A one-page printed comparison showing a sample QBDT pay stub and the equivalent IES pay stub side by side. Every line annotated: "This line moved here." "This label changed to this." "This amount is the same." Available in English and Spanish. Given to every Tier 5 employee before their first IES paycheck. The goal: when the IES stub arrives, it looks familiar, not alien. Familiarity is the outcome of this sheet. The sheet is not a manual.
📅
Format 6 · W-2 advance notice
January: "Your W-2 comes from IES this year"
The only other Tier 5 education touchpoint is in January. A one-page note (with the first IES W-2 if mailed, or text message if mobile) explaining: "Your W-2 this year comes from Intuit Enterprise Suite, not from [old system]. The format is slightly different. All the same boxes. Your accountant can use it exactly as before." For split-year migrations (QBDT + IES in same tax year): W-2 reconciliation note explaining why the total might appear in two statements.
05 — Channel matrix — right format for the right context
Delivery design

Every tier and every trigger moment requires a channel decision. The channel must match the person's physical context, device access, literacy level, and the cognitive load they have available at that moment. A field supervisor on a scaffolding at 7am on a Tuesday does not receive a portal notification — they receive a 30-second conversation with their foreman and a laminated card. A Controller at their desk on a Thursday morning receives a structured module with real data. Same concept. Completely different delivery.

06 — Wave-aligned rollout — education tied to go-live, not a calendar
Sequenced delivery

Education rollout is not a standalone programme — it is synchronised to the three migration waves. Tier 1 and 2 education runs 4–6 weeks ahead of each wave's go-live. Tier 4 and 5 education runs 1 week before and Day 0 of each wave's go-live. The sequencing prevents cognitive overload: people only receive education for the systems they are about to use, not systems that go live in 8 months.

07 — Language, literacy, and access — the equity layer
Inclusion by design

Meridian's field workforce includes Spanish-speaking employees, workers with limited written literacy, and workers without smartphones or reliable data access. An education strategy that does not account for this is not a strategy for Meridian — it is a strategy for the subset of Meridian that already has the easiest relationship with technology. The DSU model must be accessible to every tier regardless of language, device, or literacy level.

Language
English + Spanish minimum
All Tier 4/5 materials (cards, comparison sheets, foreman scripts, 90-sec videos) produced in English and Spanish. For CA and NY branches with significant populations: Mandarin and Cantonese versions of the pay stub comparison sheet considered. Not a translation — a cultural adaptation. The foreman who speaks their crew's language delivers the message. HR provides the material; the foreman chooses the words.
Literacy
Visual-first for Tier 4/5
The pay stub comparison sheet and the reference card prioritise visual comparison over written explanation. Side-by-side stub images with colour-coded annotations. Numbers, not paragraphs. The laminated card has QR codes that open a 90-second video — not a written guide. For employees who are stronger visual than written learners, the image is the education and the foreman's voice is the explanation.
Device access
Designed for the phone they have
IES self-service is mobile-first. QR codes take users directly to their specific self-service page — no navigation required. For Tier 5 workers without smartphones: the IES pay stub is printed and distributed the same way the QBDT stub was — no self-service required from them. The education for this group is entirely oral and physical. Not every person needs to touch IES. The paycheck is the product.
Data access
Offline-capable at Tier 4/5
Job sites in Arizona and rural Texas have unreliable connectivity. The reference card works offline. The foreman script works offline. The printed comparison sheet works offline. For Tier 4 employees who have smartphones but limited data: the 90-second benefit video is available for download from the Champion's QR card so it can be viewed offline. The education does not assume a reliable data connection for anyone outside an office.
08 — Measuring education success — per tier, per wave
Success metrics

Education measurement is not module completion rates. Completion is a proxy metric that organisations use because the real metric — behavioural change — is harder to collect. For each tier, the success metric is a behavioural observation or a data point, not a completion record. The question is always: "Has this person changed how they work because of what they learned?" — not "Did they finish the module?"

The programme-level education success metric is a single number: the percentage of Meridian's 210 employees who, at 90 days post full go-live, would describe their relationship with IES (directly or through its outputs) as "working correctly for them." This includes Tier 5 workers who never log in — for them, "working correctly" means their paycheck is right and they know who to call if it isn't. That is a successful education outcome. The programme is not complete until this number is ≥90% across all five tiers.
